The ‚ÄúObsessions‚Äù exhibition at Abu Rummaneh Cultural Center presents a collective artistic experience, featuring seven artists representing different artistic schools and techniques ‚Äî from realism and graphic art to Islamic art and Ajami decoration ‚Äî while exploring light, shadow, and color as expressive elements. Group Exhibitions as Visual Nourishment for the Artist

The president of the Syrian Plastic Artists Union, Mohammad Sobhi Al-Sayed Yahya, stated to SANA that the exhibition reflects seven unique impressions, each with its own beautiful color harmony. He emphasized the importance of group exhibitions in enriching themes, cultures, techniques, and artistic schools, allowing artists to learn from each other's experiences ‚Äî providing visual nourishment for every artist. He also indicated upcoming plans to hold large-scale group exhibitions bringing together all artistic styles across Syria.

Abandoned Corners and Sensory Snapshots

Artist Fawaz Al-Bayebi focuses on emotion-driven moments in his paintings, using canvas and oil colors. His works stand out by capturing deep angles and hidden ideas ‚Äî ranging from European natural landscapes to a flowing stream, a tree branch, and a moment of challenge in the eyes of a girl ‚Äî reflecting the uniqueness of each scene through vivid colors and precise composition.

Artistic Concepts Between Realism and Intuition

Artist Mohammad Dabour‚Äôs paintings are characterized by classical realism, featuring natural roses, Arabic calligraphy, and horse portraits inspired by pharaonic drawing techniques, using layered fabric and oil color. Artist Ali Al-Saadi presented paintings showcasing the beauty of nature, old Damascus, pomegranates, and a white cat, using acrylic on canvas. Meanwhile, artist Mohammad Zein Kouki focused on the reflection of shadows in sunset paintings over the sea, under a bridge, and in an alley in Cordoba, using autumn-toned oils on various surfaces. Artist Mowaffaq Al-Masri blended realism with graphic elements, depicting themes such as the martyr child Hamza Al-Khatib, freedom, peace, family, autumn, and expressive human faces, primarily painted on wood.

Damascus Inspirations, Islamic Art, and Ajami Artist Mohammad Ghoneim contributed nine paintings belonging to three artistic styles, including vegetal Ajami ornamentation and heritage-inspired pieces combining decorative patterns, Arabic script, and the Damascene lantern. He also exhibited a range of landscape works using oil, acrylic, undercoat, and lacquer. Artist Enal Al-Qaed drew inspiration from Islamic art, using calligraphic shapes, geometric patterns, and earthy heritage colors, applying collage, oil, and acrylic techniques. Audience reactions varied, ranging from admiration of artistic precision to contemplation of the symbolic, mysterious messages in some works. The exhibition, opened yesterday evening, continues until next Thursday.
